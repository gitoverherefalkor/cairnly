# Cairnly Encore: Distribution & Marketing Strategy

**Date:** 2026-07-07
**For:** Sjoerd (Cairnly / Falkor Atlas)
**Product:** Cairnly Encore, an honest AI-driven career and life-direction assessment for people at or approaching retirement. EUR 79, one-time. Live (build-complete) at cairnly.io/encore, pending the launch checklist.
**Status of this doc:** Strategy, grounded in a research pass (market, competitors, pricing, channels, messaging). Sources and confidence flags are in Appendix B. Nothing here is committed; it is a plan to react to.

---

## 1. Executive summary

The one-sentence strategy: **do not try to buy 65-year-olds with cold ads. Reach them through the people they already trust (their adult children, their financial advisor, their former employer) and through content that earns the category, then let a warm, honest assessment convert them.**

Six things that shape everything below:

1. **The white space is real.** No product anywhere combines an AI-driven personality and career assessment, a paid one-time consumer report, an AI coach chat, and explicit targeting of the 58-72 professional. Encore is close to category-defining. The flip side: there is no existing market language to ride, so we have to teach the category, not just win share in it.
2. **The audience is affluent and skeptical in equal measure, and hard to reach cold.** Only about 8% of over-65s have ever tried an AI chatbot. They have been actively warned by the FTC and AARP about online quizzes. Cold digital acquisition to this group is expensive and low-trust. This is the central GTM constraint.
3. **So the money is in borrowed trust and gifting, not paid search.** Three warm engines beat one cold one: the Gift engine (adult children, who are digital-native and are literally Cairnly's existing pro audience), the Trust-Borrow engine (financial advisors, pension funds, outplacement, 50+ orgs), and the Authority engine (SEO plus LinkedIn plus earned media plus a free lead magnet).
4. **EUR 79 is an acquisition price, not a value-captured price.** It sits roughly 2x above cheap digital peers (CliftonStrengths at USD 50, 16Personalities at EUR 29) but 10x to 80x below every human service this audience already pays for (retirement coaching at 150-300 per session, Modern Elder Academy retreats at 4,000-7,000, executive outplacement at 899-6,499 per person). That gap is an opportunity and a warning: too cheap can signal "low value" to an affluent skeptic, and a one-time EUR 79 leaves thin lifetime value. Plan the monetization beyond the first sale (Section 7).
5. **The niche is the strategy.** Current general-population data says money, not purpose, is the top reason older people return to work. That is not our audience. The affluent, college-educated professional is precisely the slice for whom purpose beats paycheck, because the paycheck is not the constraint. Market purpose only to the people for whom it is true.
6. **Heads up on the name.** "Encore" as a term is already owned in the US by Encore.org (rebranded to CoGenerate in 2022) and by the still-running Encore Fellowships program. This is not a competitive threat, but it is a brand-clarity and SEO risk. Always use the locked lockup "Cairnly Encore," and see Section 11.

## 2. The strategic situation (five hard truths, with the numbers)

**Truth 1: The market is large, growing, and wealthy.**
Retirement-age populations across our target geographies are big and the "keep working in some form" intent is now mainstream. T. Rowe Price's December 2025 Global Retirement Savers Study (covering US, UK, Canada, Australia) found 34% of savers globally, and 37% in the US, expect to work at least part-time in retirement. Canada's 65-plus labour-force participation hit a record 15.2% in 2025. In the UK roughly one in four retirees "unretire," most within five years. On wealth, Cerulli projects roughly USD 124 trillion transferring by 2048, with Boomers alone above USD 53 trillion. The affluent professional at retirement is not a niche of a niche; it is a large, liquid, underserved segment.

**Truth 2: They are the hardest audience to reach with cold digital.**
Only about 20% of 50-64 year-olds and 8% of 65-plus have tried an AI chatbot. Roughly 69% of older adults are uncomfortable sharing personal information with an AI tool, and the reasons are specific: they prefer a human explaining things (63%), they fear data being sold (61%), they are unsure how information will be used (58%). The FTC and AARP have run public campaigns warning this exact demographic about quiz-based data harvesting. A cold "take our AI quiz" ad fights all of this at once. This is why the plan leads with warm channels.

**Truth 3: Nobody credible is doing what Encore does, but the neighbours validate the demand.**
The adjacent world proves people pay for "who am I now" work: Modern Elder Academy fills 4,000-7,000 dollar retreats; retirement coaches charge 150-300 per session and package engagements into the thousands; executive outplacement runs 899-6,499 per departing person (employer-paid). The direct-competitor lane (AI plus assessment plus report plus retiree focus) is empty. The one profile-first analogue found is KWIEK in the Netherlands (values and skills profile to matched 65-plus roles). Watch it closely because it is in the home market (Section 11).

**Truth 4: The buyer and the user are sometimes different people, and that is a gift (literally).**
The strongest warm channel is the adult child (40-55) buying for a retiring parent. They are digitally reachable, emotionally motivated (watching a parent approach an identity rupture is real and it makes them feel helpless), and they overlap heavily with Cairnly's existing pro audience. Retirement gifting already favours experiences over objects. Encore fits that category exactly: it is not a mug, it is a considered process.

**Truth 5: Price is doing brand work, in both directions.**
Against cheap quizzes, EUR 79 reads premium and serious. Against the coaching and retreat world, it reads almost free. The number only works if the anchor does the lifting ("less than half of one hour with a career coach, yours to revisit for as long as you need"). Left unanchored, EUR 79 risks reading as "cheap enough to be a scam" to a former CFO. Anchor upward, never downward.

## 3. Positioning: the category and the wedge

**Category we are creating:** honest direction for your next chapter. Not retirement planning (owned by finance, and it is about money). Not senior services (condescending, and we will never use that word). The closest true peers are life-design programs (Modern Elder Academy, Designing Your Life) and retirement coaches, and we are the accessible, private, honest, on-demand version of that.

**Positioning statement (internal):**
For the accomplished professional who is ending a long career and is not done contributing, Cairnly Encore is an honest assessment that reads your real career and shows where your experience still fits, spanning work, board seats, mentoring, teaching, and building something of your own. Unlike coaching (expensive, slow, intimidating) or retirement planning (only about the money), Encore gives you a straight, private, expert-grade read in an evening, for the price of a nice dinner.

**The wedge to launch on:** the gift. It is the cheapest to acquire (borrowed trust from both Cairnly and the child), it uses assets we already have (the pro audience, the checkout, the brand), and it sidesteps the cold-reach problem entirely. Everything else builds while the gift engine proves demand.

**The through-line message (already live on the landing page, and it is good):** "You finished the career. You are not finished." Keep it. It passes every test in Section 4.

## 4. The messaging system

### The four pillars
1. **We take the loss seriously.** You are not searching for an identity, you are losing one that defined you for decades. We treat that as the real thing it is.
2. **Your experience is the asset, especially now.** Decades of judgment do not depreciate. In an AI world drowning in fast, shallow answers, seasoned judgment is getting rarer and more valuable.
3. **No condescension, no clichés, straight answers.** Including the hard truths: where age will be a real headwind, where it will be your edge, what board liability actually means.
4. **This is a direction, not a job board.** Work, advisory, a board seat, mentoring, teaching, volunteering with substance, or building your own thing. Paid or not.

### The promise (put it on the page, early)
"No jargon, no '60 is the new 40,' no golf brochures. Just a straight look at what is next." Naming the thing they fear (being patronised) and disclaiming it is the most disarming move available.

### Language: banned vs power
**Never use:** senior, senior citizen, elderly, golden years, twilight years, silver, greying, "young at heart," "still sharp / still got it / still relevant" (the word "still" is almost always the tell), "60 is the new 40," "slowing down," "reinvent yourself" (implies the current self is broken), "starting over," "finally have time for."
**Use and test:** next chapter, what is next, your next move, your experience is the asset, decades of judgment, built (not started), direction, clarity, honest, straight answers, "on your terms," "for people who do not need convincing they matter."
**The test for every line:** would a sharp, accomplished 65-year-old feel talked down to by this? If a sentence only works because it is said to someone older, cut it.

### How to introduce the AI (this matters)
Do not lead with "AI." Lead with the outcome (clarity on what is next) and the promise (honesty). Introduce AI only as the mechanism, once, in plain language: "An assessment built on your actual answers, using AI to spot patterns across a whole career that a single conversation cannot." Never claim the AI "knows you." The honest and higher-converting frame: "It knows patterns across thousands of profiles. It does not know you. Only you know you. This is a mirror, not a verdict." Keep a visible human escape hatch ("a question about your report? A person answers."). Show your work in the report ("you said X and Y, which is why we flagged Z"), which does more for AI trust in this age group than any reassurance copy.

### Objection handling (the honest counters)
| Objection | Honest counter |
|---|---|
| "Another online quiz." | Do not argue the label, out-build it: show the depth (real assessment, written report, follow-up conversation) before asking for payment. Legitimacy is demonstrated, not asserted. |
| "I don't need a job." | Agree, then reframe: "Good, because this is not about a job. It is about where four decades of judgment are worth pointing next, paid or not." Never use job-search language on this flavor. |
| "AI can't know me." | Agree completely: "You are right. It does not. What it does is show you patterns in your own answers that are hard to see from inside your own head." |
| "I'll figure it out myself." | Do not contest their capability, contest the cost of doing it alone: "You probably could, over months. This gets you there in an evening, then you do the real figuring." |
| "Is my data safe?" | One visible plain-language paragraph: answers are never sold, here is who sees what, one payment and no subscription ever, here is a real human to email. Billing safety and data safety are the same anxiety to this buyer. |
| "Why should I trust you to build this?" | A real, named team and methodology page. This objection is defused by a page existing, not by clever copy. |

### Ten do's and don'ts (pin these to the wall)
1. Do lead with the real moment before mentioning AI or the product category.
2. Don't ever write "still" into a sentence about capability or relevance.
3. Do name their skepticism out loud before they voice it.
4. Don't use age-erasure math.
5. Do explain the AI mechanism in one plain paragraph before asking for payment.
6. Don't claim the AI "knows" the user.
7. Do anchor EUR 79 against a real coaching-hour rate, and say "one payment, no subscription" out loud.
8. Don't cent-price it (no 78.99) or anchor it against free quizzes.
9. Do frame the gift around the child's pride in the parent's past, not worry about the parent's future.
10. Don't use countdown timers, live-viewer counts, or any manufactured urgency. This audience detects manipulation fastest of any segment, and it contradicts the whole brand.

## 5. The three-engine model (the heart of the plan)

Encore should run on three engines that feed each other. Lead with Gift (cheapest, warmest, uses what we have), build Authority in parallel (compounds, feeds the other two), and layer in Trust-Borrow as the scale engine (slower, highest ceiling).

### Engine 1: Gift (B2C, warm): the launch wedge
**Why it wins:** the buyer (adult child, 40-55) is digitally reachable and already overlaps Cairnly's pro base. Trust is doubly borrowed: the child trusts Cairnly, the parent trusts the child. Lowest CAC available to us.
**The emotional frame (get this exactly right):** investment in their next win, never rescue from decline. "You have watched her build an entire career. Give her the space to figure out what she builds next, on her terms." Never "worried about mom's retirement?"
**Occasions to build around:** the retirement party, the 65th birthday, and the strongest and most underused one, "the Sunday night before the first Monday," the first time in decades that Monday means nothing. One sharp refinement from the research: the acute "who am I now" pain does not peak at the party, it peaks roughly six to twenty-four months in, once the honeymoon of free time fades (this is the disenchantment-and-reorientation window in the standard retirement-transition model). The pre-retirement window is saturated by financial planners; the year-or-two-in window is comparatively unclaimed and is when someone is most ready to pay for direction. So run two gift moments: one at the retirement date, and a second "a few months in, when the calendar is quiet" nudge, which may convert better.
**What to build:** a separate gift flow and landing page (/encore/gift) that talks to the child about the parent; a real reveal artifact (a card or certificate, not just an activation code in a receipt); optional scheduled delivery (buy now, deliver on retirement day); a personal note field; and reassurance for the giver that the parent's answers stay private, even from them.
**First move:** cross-sell the gift to Cairnly's existing pro customers and email list. They already trust the brand and many have a parent in range.

### Engine 2: Trust-Borrow (B2B2C, partnerships): the scale engine
**Why it wins:** it solves the cold-reach problem by renting an intermediary's existing trust and audience, and it reaches the buyer at exactly the high-intent moment.
**The sharpest wedge: financial advisors and wealth managers.** Retirement is the single moment a client is most likely to disengage or move assets. Advisors know it and are not resourced for the "now what do I do with myself" conversation. Encore lets them add that value without becoming a life coach. The pitch is retention and referral, not wellness. Deal shapes: affiliate referral with revenue share, co-branded gift the advisor hands to retiring clients, or bulk gift codes as a client-appreciation benefit. Trust fit is clean (EU servers, GDPR, no subscription, one-click delete), so nothing embarrasses a regulated firm.
**The single warmest sub-segment is already trained to do exactly this by hand: the "life planning" advisor community.** George Kinder's Kinder Institute has trained 600-plus Registered Life Planners in 30 countries in a values-first, "if money were not a constraint, what would you do" discovery method, and the Dutch FFP (roughly 3,000 certified planners) publishes the same "financial lifestyle planning" thesis. These advisors run the Encore conversation manually today and have no digital tool for it. Encore is the tool they are describing. This is the lowest-friction, highest-trust, and NL-relevant partner channel, and it should be the first B2B pilot. See the named target list in Appendix C.
**Other trust-borrow targets, in rough priority:** pension providers with a documented "non-financial readiness" wedge (Aviva's Mid-Life MOT already has a "Work" pillar; the NN and Empower content already concede purpose matters); outplacement firms (Randstad RiseSmart's "Creative Retirement" is a named product and Randstad is NL-headquartered, so Encore as a pre-coaching intake step is a warm pitch; LHH and Korn Ferry serve the senior-exec transition at thousands per person); 50-plus membership organisations (UK Rest Less has a million members and no assessment of its own; NL's KBO-PCOB runs an explicit "Zingeving"/meaning pillar; AARP's May 2025 Indeed "Career Hub" deal is a direct precedent for a paid third-party service sold to members).
**Board work as an upstream play:** programs like the FT NED Diploma (GBP 5,500) and IMD Board Readiness (CHF 22,500) and marketplaces like Nurole (free to candidates, and its new "Nurole Plus" tier is already adding coaching for aspiring NEDs) sit downstream of Encore. Encore is the "should I even go down the board road, among several paths" step before someone spends five figures. Nurole is the most natural affiliate (no pricing conflict, they monetise the employer side).
**First move:** founder-led outreach to the Kinder/FFP life-planning community and one 50-plus org for a pilot. This is a relationship sale; start it now, it has the longest lead time. The full named-target list with temperatures is in Appendix C.

### Engine 3: Authority (content, organic, direct): the compounding engine
**Why it wins:** it builds the category language we have to create anyway, it captures the high-intent search that does exist, and it produces the trust assets (testimonials, a real team page, useful guides) that make the other two engines convert.
**The free lead magnet:** a short "Next Chapter Reality Check" (8 questions, an honest one-page archetype read, no payment) that gives value before asking for money, proves the real product is deeper than a quiz, seeds the retargeting audience, and doubles as the free thing partners can hand out.
**SEO:** own long-tail, high-intent, low-competition themes: "how to become a non-executive director," "encore careers what they pay," "purpose after retirement," "advisory work after a corporate career," "using your experience in an AI world." Each ends with the Reality Check, which bridges to Encore.
**LinkedIn:** founder-led thought leadership aimed at both the pre-retiree executive and the adult child. It is the one platform where we can target 55-plus senior titles and their 40-55 children in the same motion. Organic first, small paid amplification second.
**Earned media and peer proof:** press or third-party mentions outperform self-description for this segment. And the single highest-leverage asset is peer testimonials with real names, faces, former titles, and ages. A named 63-year-old former CFO outweighs five anonymous younger reviews. Prioritise collecting these from the first cohort.
**Paid's real job:** supporting, not leading. Use Meta and LinkedIn for retargeting warm traffic and lookalikes off the email list, not cold prospecting at 65-year-olds. Calm, premium creative, a real face around 60, no stock-happy nonsense, no urgency gimmicks.

## 7. Pricing and the lifetime-value problem (read this before scaling spend)

EUR 79 is the right launch price for the wedge, but be clear-eyed about what it is: a low-friction entry price, not a price that captures the value of the guidance. The research is unambiguous. Against digital peers it is premium (about 1.7x CliftonStrengths at USD 50, about 2.7x 16Personalities at EUR 29). Against every human alternative this audience already buys, it is 10x to 80x cheaper (coaching 150-300 per session, MEA retreats 4,000-7,000, outplacement 899-6,499, a financial advisor at roughly 1% of assets, which is about 10,000 a year on a 1M portfolio).

Two implications:

1. **A one-time EUR 79 with a hard-to-reach audience makes cold paid acquisition math difficult.** If CAC on cold ads to this segment runs high (it will), a single EUR 79 sale may not clear it. This is the quantitative reason the plan leads with gift, partnership, and organic, where effective CAC is far lower.
2. **Plan the second and third euro now.** Options to extend lifetime value, in rough order of ease:
   - **The coaching-hour anchor, stated explicitly.** Do this regardless. It is the cheapest lever and it protects the price from reading as "too cheap to be real."
   - **A premium tier (test EUR 149-249)** that bundles the assessment with a live human coaching session delivered by a partner coach. This captures the real willingness to pay, creates affiliate revenue, and gives the affluent skeptic the human touch they told us they want.
   - **An annual "check-in" renewal.** A portfolio life evolves; a yearly re-read at a modest price turns a one-time buyer into a recurring one without a subscription feel.
   - **Referral and gift multipliers.** A satisfied user is a natural gifter (a friend also retiring) and a natural referrer. Build the loop.
   - **B2B volume deals.** The real scale monetisation: advisors, employers, and orgs buying codes in bulk, where the per-unit price can hold and the CAC is amortised across a relationship.

Do not relitigate the EUR 79 launch decision. Do decide, before spending on acquisition, which of these extensions ships first. My recommendation: ship the coaching-hour anchor immediately (copy only), stand up the gift and referral loops in Phase 1, and pilot the premium human-coaching tier in Phase 2.

## 8. Phased roadmap

**Phase 0 (now to launch, weeks 0-4): clear the runway and warm-launch.**
Finish the four launch blockers (activate WF1P-WF4P, set the webhook secret, review survey copy, smoke test). Build the gift flow and /encore/gift page. Ship the free Reality Check lead magnet. **Phase 1 (months 1-3): Gift plus Authority.**
Cross-sell the gift to the pro base. Launch the LinkedIn thought-leadership cadence and the SEO pillar plus first cluster articles. Begin founder outreach to 5-10 financial advisors and one 50-plus org. Turn on modest retargeting off the email list. Goal: demonstrate warm-channel demand, refine messaging against real objections, collect 10-plus strong testimonials, sign the first partner pilot.

**Phase 2 (months 3-9): add Trust-Borrow.**
Formalise the advisor partner program (pick affiliate or co-brand based on Phase 1 signal). Pursue one pension-fund or larger 50-plus org pilot and one employer/outplacement pilot. Pilot the premium human-coaching tier. Expand content, push PR on the category and founder story. Goal: a repeatable partner motion and the first institutional deal.

**Phase 3 (months 9-18): scale what works.**
Double down on the engine with the best CAC-to-value ratio. Expand geographically (UK and US are the largest English markets; the NL home market is where KWIEK competes, so defend it). Add the renewal or check-in layer. Goal: predictable acquisition and a monetisation model that is not dependent on a single EUR 79 sale.

## 9. Metrics and unit economics

**Track from day one:**
- CAC by channel (the whole point of the three-engine thesis is that gift, partner, and organic CAC should sharply beat cold-paid CAC; prove it).
- Assessment completion rate (a paid user who does not finish is a refund and a lost testimonial).
- Report-to-coach-chat engagement (the coach chat is a retention and satisfaction signal).
- Testimonial and referral rate (the compounding trust asset).
- Gift share of sales and gift redemption rate (redemption lag is real for gifts; watch it).
- Partner-sourced share of sales (the scale signal).

**Framing targets, not promises:** for a warm gift or partner sale, effective CAC should sit well under EUR 79, so the first sale can be roughly break-even to positive on its own. For cold paid, assume it will not clear on a single sale, which is why cold paid is a retargeting and list-building tool, not an acquisition engine, until a lifetime-value extension (Section 7) is live. Do not scale any channel whose CAC exceeds near-term realisable value per customer.

## 10. Budget scenarios

**Lean (bootstrapped, founder time is the main input):**
Engines: Gift cross-sell, Authority (organic LinkedIn plus SEO plus lead magnet), founder-led advisor outreach. Paid spend limited to small retargeting. This is mostly sweat and content. It is the right default given the audience rewards trust over spend, and cold paid is weak here anyway.

**Funded (if there is a marketing budget to deploy):**
Add: a testimonial and case-study production sprint (this segment's #1 conversion asset), a PR push on the category and founder story, a part-time partnerships person to work the advisor and org pipeline in parallel, and a content production cadence faster than founder-only allows. Put very little into cold paid; put it into trust production and partnership capacity instead. The constraint on this business is trust and reach, not creative volume.

## 11. Risks and mitigations

- **Reachability (the big one).** Hardest audience to acquire cold. Mitigation: the entire warm-first, three-engine plan. Do not fight this with ad budget.
- **Thin lifetime value on a one-time EUR 79.** Mitigation: the Section 7 extensions, decided before scaling spend.
- **Legitimacy for a young unknown brand selling to skeptical affluent olders.** Mitigation: named peer testimonials, a real team and methodology page, partner trust-borrowing, PR, and the plain-language data and AI paragraphs.
- **The gifting landmine ("are you saying I'm old and lost?").** Mitigation: pride-not-worry framing, and explicit privacy reassurance for both parent and child.
- **AI distrust.** Mitigation: AI as mechanism not headline, never "it knows you," visible human contact, show-your-work reports.
- **The name.** "Encore" is already associated with Encore.org/CoGenerate and the Encore Fellowships program in the US, and "encore career" is a coined term with existing SEO weight. Mitigation: never use "Encore" bare; always the "Cairnly Encore" lockup. Own the term hard in your own markets and channels. Do a trademark check before heavy spend on the name. Treat CoGenerate's SEO as a reason to compete on long-tail intent phrases ("what to do after retiring," "advisory work after a corporate career") rather than the head term "encore career," which is contested.
- **KWIEK (NL).** The one profile-first analogue, in the home market, targeting 65-plus. Mitigation: do a hands-on trial, position Encore's depth (full assessment plus report plus coach chat, not just job matching) against their lighter matching model, and defend NL deliberately in Phase 3.
- **MyPassion.AI (the one AI competitor to watch).** A freemium AI career-assessment tool with an archetype model and a paid premium tier. It does not target retirees today (its stated personas skew 28-38), but its "50-plus" guidance already name-checks encore, advisory, and instructional roles, and it has the AI infrastructure to launch a retiree SKU fast. Mitigation: move first and own the niche and the trust assets; monitor its roadmap. Also note AARP's free "Life Reimagined" style tools are the zero-price US comparison a prospect may reach for, so the paid pitch must answer "why pay when AARP is free" with depth, personalisation, and the coach chat.
- **Purpose-versus-money narrative risk.** If we market "purpose" to the general retiree population, current data (money-driven unretirement) undercuts us. Mitigation: stay disciplined on the affluent-professional niche, where purpose genuinely leads.

## Appendix B: research sources and confidence flags

This strategy rests on a research pass. Use the numbers, but note the caveats before quoting any in public.

**High confidence (recent, primary or well-established):**
- T. Rowe Price Global Retirement Savers Study, Dec 2025: 34% global / 37% US of savers expect to work in retirement (US, UK, Canada, Australia).
- Cerulli: roughly USD 124 trillion wealth transfer through 2048, Boomers above USD 53 trillion (US). An earlier Cerulli figure of USD 84 trillion through 2045 is the more commonly cited older number.
- Statistics Canada, 2025/2026: 65-plus labour-force participation at a record 15.2%; about 10% of ever-retired 55-plus working in 2023.
- UK ONS / Standard Life (2026): roughly 100,000 unretired in the year to March 2023; about one in six retirees have returned or are considering it.
- AI adoption (AARP): about 20% of 50-64 and 8% of 65-plus have tried an AI chatbot; about 69% of older adults uncomfortable sharing personal data with an AI tool.
- Pricing peers (verified live): CliftonStrengths full report USD 49.99; 16Personalities Premium Career Suite EUR 29; Randstad RiseSmart outplacement USD 899-6,499 per person; Modern Elder Academy retreats about USD 4,000-7,000 (site fetch blocked, figure via search snippet, treat as good-not-perfect).
- Ad trust by age (UK Advertising Association / Credos, 2024): over-55s trust online ads at 14% (vs 51% for 18-34) and social/influencer ads at 7%, and Ofcom (2025) finds this age group is better than average at spotting sponsored content and scams. This is the hard backbone for "do not lead with cold paid social."
- Search beats social for this segment's purchase intent: roughly 58% feel ready to buy after search research vs about 24-30% after social/video, and only about 8% trust social ads for recommendations (Disruptive Digital / Statista synthesis). Treat search and SEO as a conversion channel here, not just awareness.
- AI usage is not AI trust: about 45% of Boomers used an AI tool in the past six months (Menlo Ventures, 2025) yet roughly 69-74% distrust AI-generated information; older users react negatively to confidence scores and trust explanations that cite their own checkable inputs (Georgia Tech, CHI 2026). This is why the report should show its work from the user's own answers and never show a confidence percentage.
- Affluence caveat (important for honest sizing): about 51% of Americans turning 65 in 2024-2030 have investable assets under USD 100,000 (Alliance for Lifetime Income). The ICP is the top 20-30% of this age band by wealth and education, not the median retiree. Size the market to the affluent slice, not the whole cohort.
- Word-of-mouth structure (East et al., 2013, peer-reviewed): older consumers receive less word-of-mouth (more socially isolated) yet weight it most heavily for big-ticket and financial decisions, and trust credentialed/institutional sources over anonymous crowd sentiment. This is the academic reason seeded, expert-framed referral (an advisor, a Rotary talk, an alumni lecture) has outsized effect for this audience.

**Use with a label (dated "landmark" stats, still widely circulated):**
- Merrill Lynch / Age Wave "Work in Retirement" (2014): 67% would work past 65 to stay mentally active; affluent-subsample youthfulness stats. Twelve years old, no confirmed refresh.
- Encore.org "9 million in encore careers, 31 million want one" (2011). Fifteen years old and pre-COVID. Do not present as current market size.

**Notable competitive findings:**
- No direct competitor (AI plus assessment plus paid report plus AI coach plus retiree focus) found. Whitespace is real, but so is the absence of ready-made category language.
- Naming collision: Encore.org (now CoGenerate) and the Encore Fellowships program own "encore" in the US market.
- KWIEK (NL) is the closest profile-first analogue and is in the home market.

**Gaps worth a follow-up before heavy investment:**
- Netherlands-specific unretirement and participation data (needs a CBS StatLine pass in Dutch).
- Non-US wealth-transfer figures (the Great Wealth Transfer framing is very US-centric).
- A hands-on trial of KWIEK, and an app-store plus Product Hunt sweep for stealth or non-English competitors that search under-indexes.
- A trademark check on "Encore" in the relevant classes and geographies.
